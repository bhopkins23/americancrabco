Everything Crab

A quick primer on Maryland crabs

The Maryland blue crab, scientifically known as Callinectes sapidus, translates beautifully to "beautiful swimmer" in Latin. And beautiful they are, with their distinctive blue claws and olive-green shells that turn brilliant red when steamed.

Blue crabs hold tremendous cultural significance throughout the Chesapeake Bay region and beyond. In Maryland, the traditional preparation method is steaming crabs with Old Bay seasoning and rock salt, creating that iconic spicy-sweet flavor profile. In other regions, particularly Louisiana and the Gulf Coast, boiling is the preferred method, often incorporating cayenne pepper and other bold spices into the cooking liquid.

Whether steamed or boiled, picking crabs is a social tradition that brings families and friends together around newspaper-covered tables, armed with mallets and knives, sharing stories between bites of sweet, delicate crabmeat.

What's So Special About Maryland Crabs?

Maryland blue crabs aren't just regionally celebrated; they're a national treasure. The Chesapeake Bay and surrounding Maryland waters account for approximately 50% of the entire United States blue crab harvest. This dominance isn't just about quantity, it's about quality.

When compared to other popular crab varieties like Dungeness or snow crab, Maryland blue crabs offer a superior flavor and texture that seafood enthusiasts can identify immediately. The meat is sweeter, more delicate, and has a distinctive texture that's both tender and satisfying.

The seasonality of blue crabs, running primarily from April through November, adds to their appreciation. During the peak summer months, crabs are building up fat stores in preparation for winter hibernation, which gives their meat that characteristic buttery, rich flavor that connoisseurs prize.

Expert tasters and crabbers can identify Maryland blue crabs by sight alone, recognizing the telltale color patterns and body proportions. But the real test comes in the taste. Once you've experienced the sweet, briny flavor of a properly steamed Maryland blue crab, you'll understand why they command such reverence.

Soft Shell Crabs

Soft shell crabs represent one of the most delicate and sought-after delicacies in the seafood world. These are blue crabs that have molted, shedding their hard outer shell, within the last 12 hours. During this brief window, the entire crab (except for the gills and abdominal parts) is completely edible, shell and all.

Blue crabs molt between 18 and 23 times during their lifetime as they grow. Each time they shed their shell, there's a narrow opportunity to harvest them as soft shells before their new shell hardens.

Experienced crabbers can identify crabs that are about to molt, called "peelers," by examining the color of the line on their swimming fin. This line progresses from white to pink to red as the molting time approaches. Once identified, these peelers are placed in special shedding tanks where they can be monitored closely and harvested immediately after molting.

Preparation and Serving

Soft shell crabs can be prepared in two primary ways: fried with a seasoned batter or sauteed in butter. While frying is more common in restaurants and produces that satisfying crispy exterior, many chefs and home cooks prefer sauteing for the way it highlights the natural sweetness of the crab without overwhelming it with breading.

The classic presentation is as a sandwich, where the soft shell crab is served on a soft roll with mayonnaise, crisp lettuce, and fresh tomato. The contrast of textures and the simple accompaniments let the delicate crab flavor shine through. Alternatively, soft shells can be served plain on a platter with just a squeeze of lemon, allowing diners to fully appreciate their unique taste and texture.
